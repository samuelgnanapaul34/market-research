Scoring 17 Agentic-AI Ideas — One Honest Ranking

Seventeen agentic-AI business ideas, each scored against the same 12-point checklist and ranked from strongest to weakest — with a plain explanation of which idea comes out on top and why. Plus ten big, well-known markets that don't make the cut, and the reason each one fails. June 2026.

This page compares a wide range of AI-agent business ideas — across retail operations, back-office services, and online-seller tools — on one consistent checklist, and ranks them. The point is a fair side-by-side: which idea is the strongest bet for a small, AI-first team to build, and the reasoning behind it. The front-runner has its own detailed write-up — The Supplier & Purchase-Order Exception Agent. The top idea is a suite of three closely-related buying tasks, sold to the same buyer, so they're grouped as one product with each part's score shown.

On this page: 1 · The checklist 2 · The full ranking (17 ideas) 3 · The five groups, explained 4 · The big markets that don't make the cut 5 · What the whole table shows

1 · The checklist — how every idea was scored

Each idea is scored from 1 to 5 on the same 12 questions, and the answers are added up two ways:

  • A simple total out of 60 — every question counts the same.
  • A weighted total out of 85 — the make-or-break questions count double, and the "easy to buy" questions count one-and-a-half times.

The make-or-break questions are the ones that actually sink small ventures: is the pain real, is it urgent, and — above all — can the team actually reach the people who would buy it? A brilliant idea with no realistic way to sell it is not a brilliant idea.

3 · The five groups, and what each one shows

① Brand tools (6 ideas · sold to online brands · the earliest set)

"Do-it-for-me" tools bolted onto a price-and-stock monitoring engine, sold to online brands and Shopify sellers. The out-of-stock "strike" ad agent (60/85) is the best of this group — when a competitor runs out of stock, it automatically boosts your ads and reaches out to customers. The rest fade because the markets are already crowded with established tools. What this group shows: the buyer here is the brand — a completely different customer from a retailer — so it informs the industry picture but doesn't carry over to the retail work.

② IT incident "root-cause" platform (1 idea · standalone · rejected)

A tool that watches software systems, finds the root cause when something breaks, and fixes it. It scores 61.5/85 but is rejected head-on — it's the most crowded, best-funded area in the whole study (one rival valued near $1 billion, plus offerings from the big cloud and monitoring companies, some bundling it in for free). What it shows: the advantage goes to whoever already holds the data — and here that's the big incumbents, not a new team. That same test later sinks the re-ordering idea too.

③ B2B back-office shortlist (6 ideas · scored once "can we reach the buyer?" became the deciding question)

The IT-services (MSP) idea looks best on paper — but only with a realistic way to reach lots of IT firms to sell to, which isn't the case here. Once the focus narrows to a market that can be reached (retail), this whole group drops down: the off-target ideas (fund admin, freight, broker submissions — several also in crowded markets) fall away.

④ Retail-ops set (the focus area · the retailer is the buyer)

Products where there's a realistic path to retail customers. The three core buying tasks — purchase-order exceptions, new-product/SKU setup, and supplier onboarding — combine into one Buying Agent suite sold to the retail buying desk, with purchase-order exception as the lead, lowest-risk starting module. Three further retail agents — re-ordering, price-change, and returns — are ranked separately, as they serve different teams (planning, pricing, operations).

⑤ The two finalists, head-to-head (the deep check)

Purchase-order exception vs. re-ordering, with the competition checked against current, real funding. The purchase-order agent holds firm at 49/60, 69/85 (Strong). The re-ordering agent drops to 42/60, 60/85 once two large planning vendors are found already shipping it as an AI agent. The purchase-order exception agent is the strongest — the full reasoning is in its own write-up.

5 · What the whole table shows

  • The strongest idea isn't the highest score — it's the highest score that passes all three tests: the field is open (no giant already owns it), the buyer can actually be reached, and the advantage builds over time for the team that builds it. The IT-services idea scores higher on paper but fails "can the buyer be reached?". The brand tools and the root-cause platform fail "is the field open?". The big famous markets fail both.
  • Most scores bunch up in the 55–70 band. Once funded competitors are taken into account, categories even out — so the deciding factor is never the headline number. It's the starting point: one specific, defensible slice a funded copycat can't easily reach. The Buying Agent's first module (the retailer's side of purchase-order exceptions, mid-size, with a realistic way in) is exactly that.
  • The same two questions did most of the cutting: "is a giant already shipping this?" (sank the re-ordering agent, the root-cause platform, bookkeeping, legal) and "does the long-term advantage go to whoever holds the data — and is that the incumbent, not us?" (sank the re-ordering agent and the root-cause platform). The Buying Agent passes both.
  • Knowing the buyer matters: three groups touch "retail / shopping," but the customer is different each time — an online brand is not a mid-size retailer. Only the retailer group lines up with a market that can actually be reached.
Bottom line: across 17 scored ideas and 10 big markets that don't make the cut, the Buying Agent — led by purchase-order exception handling — is the single option that is open, reachable, and compounding all at once. Everything else either scored lower, had no realistic way to reach its buyers, or walked straight into a well-funded competitor.
