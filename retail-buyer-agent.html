01The problem — what a buying team's day really looks like

A retailer's buying team places thousands of orders with suppliers and looks after thousands of products. A huge amount of that work is repetitive, manual, and never-ending. Three jobs eat most of the time:

  • Orders come back wrong. Suppliers ship less than ordered, charge a different price, deliver late, or never confirm. Someone has to spot each problem and chase the supplier by email and phone. A single 100-line order can take an hour or more to sort out — and there are hundreds of suppliers.
  • New products take ages to set up. When a supplier sends a new range, someone keys each product into the system by hand — description, codes, price, category. It's slow and error-prone, and products miss their on-shelf date.
  • New suppliers are slow to bring on. Collecting documents, checking insurance and compliance, and setting the supplier up in the system is all manual chasing.
The retailer's main system (its ERP — usually SAP) shows these problems but doesn't do anything about them. So people do it all by hand, every day.

02What the Retail Buyer Agent does

It takes over the buying desk's manual work — the hands-on jobs a buying team grinds through every day — doing each one end-to-end for a person to approve, inside the system they already use. No re-keying, no new system to learn. We ship the most painful job first, then add the rest on the same foundation.

★

Purchase-order exceptions — the beachhead we ship first

It reads every incoming purchase order against what the supplier actually delivered, spots the mismatch (short, late, wrong price, missing confirmation), chases the supplier to resolution, and updates the system — clawing back overpayments and stopping stockouts. We land here because it's the most painful job, the easiest to prove with a money-back audit, and the one no funded rival owns.

…on the way to running the whole buying desk:

Sets up new products

Turns a supplier's product sheet into ready-to-approve SKU records — hours per product become seconds.

Onboards new suppliers

Collects documents, checks insurance & compliance certificates, sets the supplier up, and chases what's missing.

Chases & settles disputes

Runs the back-and-forth with suppliers on claims, short-pays and penalties, through to resolution.

Tracks vendor performance

Records on-time / in-full and reliability and writes up the supplier scorecard — it reports, it doesn't decide.

Handles cost-price & rebate admin

Processes price changes and claims back the rebate and co-op money the retailer is owed.

Sets up promotions

Builds the promo orders and supplier-funded deal sheets, ready to approve.

The boundary that keeps it winnable: the agent does the work; it doesn't make the buying calls. It deliberately stays out of deciding what to order, forecasting demand, or choosing suppliers — that's what the big planning systems (RELEX, Blue Yonder, Coupa) already do. It does the hands-on jobs those systems leave to people, where a buying team is drowning and the big tools are weak.

03Who buys it

The buyer

  • One clear owner: the Head of Buying (or Supply Chain Director) runs the whole buying desk — so there's one obvious person to sell to.
  • The team that feels the pain: the buyers and buying-admin staff who do the manual chasing today.

The kind of retailer

  • Mid-sized retailers and wholesalers (roughly $50M–$5B in sales).
  • Run a real ERP system (such as SAP) and deal with lots of suppliers.
  • Too small for the giant enterprise tools, but big enough to feel the pain badly.

The opening: no well-funded company owns the mid-sized, retailer-side version of this. It's open precisely because it's narrow and unglamorous — and the more the agent runs, the more it learns about how each supplier's problems get fixed, which is hard for a newcomer to copy.

06How to build it — and where to start

Don't try to build all three jobs at once. Start with the one that's cheapest to prove and faces no real competitor: purchase-order exceptions (PO exceptions).

To be clear: the product is the agent that does the work — the PO-exception handling described above. The "money-back audit" below isn't the product; it's just the easiest way to win the first customer, by showing them how much these problems are already costing.

The way in: a money-back audit

  • Read a few months of the retailer's orders and show them, in pounds, exactly how much these problems are costing — a number most teams can't work out for themselves.
  • It's a low-risk first "yes" (you're just showing them the money). Then the agent that recovers it is an easy next step.
  • Once you're in, add product-setup and supplier-onboarding to the same customer.

How it's built

  • Connect read-only first — the agent watches the orders and shipments without changing anything. Low risk, easy for IT to allow.
  • Propose, then act — the agent drafts the supplier chase for a person to approve, then takes over once it's trusted.
  • The tricky part is connecting to the retailer's system — which is also what makes it hard for others to copy.

07Timeline

0–3 months · the audit. Connect read-only; show a retailer the money they're losing; sign up 3 early partners.
3–6 months · the agent. Turn on automatic order-chasing for the first paying retailers.
6–12 months · scale. Add a second system connector; reach 15–30 retailers.
12–24 months · the full assistant. Add the next buying-desk jobs (product setup, onboarding, claims, vendor scorecards, promotions); reach 80–115 retailers and ~£4–5M a year.
