Explainer · Two opposing views, in simple language

Will AI Agents Finish Off SaaS?

Two of the sharpest write-ups on this question — Foundation Capital's "Context Graph" view and Mr. Girish Mathrubootham's "Build It Fresh for AI" view — fully explained, side by side, in plain words.

In February 2026, Anthropic (the company behind Claude) released a product called Claude Cowork. Within 48 hours, more than $285 billion of value was wiped off the share prices of software companies. People started fearing a "SaaSpocalypse" — i.e. the death of the software industry as we know it. Two important essays came out of this fear. Kindly note: both writers actually agree on the basic diagnosis. They differ only on one point — where the real, long-lasting business advantage will sit in the new world. This page lays out both views, fully and simply.

1 · Where both of them agree (Mr. Girish Mathrubootham of Freshworks & Foundation Capital — Jaya Gupta & Ashu Garg)

Before they differ, both writers agree on four points. As of 2026, this is more or less the settled view among serious people in the industry:

① AI agents will not finish off the "system of record"

The idea that "Claude will replace Salesforce" is mistaken. An agent still needs a reliable master database — canonical data — to work on. The brain still needs a nervous system

② Simply pasting AI on top of old software is a dead end

An "AI layer" added on a 2015-era database is only a wrapper — a temporary patch, not the real winner.

③ Moving from "software you operate" to "software that does the work"

Both use almost this exact phrase. The product stops being a tool that a human runs, and becomes a worker that acts on its own.

④ Basic building blocks are not the same as a finished product

Anyone can now join together the AI model, a database and some connectors. But that is only the plumbing — not a defensible business.
The real question both are answering: if SaaS is not killed by one AI release, then who or what will actually disturb it — and what is the one valuable, ownable asset in this new world? This is exactly where their answers separate.
VIEW A · Foundation Capital (a US venture capital firm) — Jaya Gupta & Ashu Garg · Dec 2025

The "Context Graph": a master record for decisions, not just data

Main point: The previous generation of giants became big by owning the data — Salesforce owns customer data, Workday owns employee data, SAP owns operations data. The next generation of giants will become big by owning the decisions — that is, the reasoning behind the decisions, which no software stores today.

The one idea the whole view rests on: "Rules" versus "Decision Traces"

A Rule — what should happen, in general

"Use the official revenue figure for reporting." "Do not give more than 10% discount on renewals." This is general policy. Existing software can store this much.

A Decision Trace — what actually happened, in this one case

"In this deal we used definition X, under policy version 3.2, with a special approval from a Vice-President, based on a similar past case Z — and here is what we changed." This is what is never stored anywhere.

So agents do not just need the rules. They need to see how the rules were actually applied in the past — where exceptions were given, how conflicts were settled, who approved what, and which past cases truly govern reality on the ground.

What today's master systems never capture

The wall that agents hit is not missing data. It is missing reasoning. Four kinds of knowledge live only in chat threads and inside people's heads — never saved as proper data:

  • Exception logic in people's heads — "We always give healthcare clients an extra 10%, because their purchase process is very slow and painful." This is not in the CRM. It is passed on by word of mouth.
  • Lessons from past decisions — "We structured a similar deal for Company X last quarter — we should stay consistent." No system links these two deals or records why.
  • Joining information across many systems — a support lead checks the customer's revenue in Salesforce, sees two open complaints in Zendesk, reads a chat message warning that the client may leave, and then decides. All this thinking happens in his head. The ticket only says "escalated to Tier 3." The reasoning is lost.
  • Approvals that happen outside any system — a Vice-President approves a discount on a Zoom call or a chat message. The record only shows the final price. It does not show who approved the deviation, or why.
What "never captured" really means: it does not mean the data is dirty or scattered. It means the reasoning that connects the data to the action was never treated as data in the first place.

A worked-out example: the renewal agent

A renewal agent proposes a 20% discount. Company policy allows only 10% on renewals, unless a special "service problem" exception is approved. So the agent does the following:
1 · CollectPulls 3 major outage incidents from the monitoring tool (PagerDuty)
→
2 · CollectFinds an open "fix it or we cancel" complaint in the support tool (Zendesk)
→
3 · Past caseFinds last quarter's similar exception approved by a VP
→
4 · RouteSends the exception to the Finance team
→
5 · ApproveFinance approves it
In the end the CRM stores only one fact: "20% discount." Everything that actually mattered — the why, the inputs, the approval, the past case — would normally be lost. But if the agent saves this full trace, the company now has something it never had before.

Do this thousands of times, and these traces join together into a "context graph" (a connected web of decisions and their reasons) — linking the things the business already cares about (accounts, renewals, tickets, incidents, policies, approvers) through the decision moments and the "why" links between them. Now the company can check, debug and replay how decisions were made — and turn each exception into a reusable past case, instead of re-learning the same thing in chat threads every quarter. Every automated decision adds one more trace. So the value keeps growing on its own.

Why the big established players cannot easily build this

Operational incumbents (Salesforce, ServiceNow, Workday)

They are built to store only the present state — they know what the deal looks like now, not what it looked like at the moment of decision. So they cannot replay the "why." Also, each one sees only its own area, so none of them sees the full cross-system picture. Their new AI features inherit the same limits

Data-warehouse players (Snowflake, Databricks)

These are "data store rooms" that only read data after the event. (Data is copied into them later through a process called ETL.) By the time the data reaches them, the decision and its context are already over. They can tell you what happened, but not why.
Why a startup has the natural advantage here: the agent is the thing doing the actual work, so it sits right at the moment of action (the "execution path") — the only place where the "why" is visible. The big players can make data harder to extract, "but they cannot insert themselves into a work-execution layer they were never part of."

Signals for a founder — a practical checklist

Many people doing it by hand

If 50 people are manually doing one task, it is a signal — the decision-making is too complex for ordinary software.

Lots of exceptions & judgement

Where "it depends" is the honest answer: deal pricing desks, loan underwriting, compliance checks, complaint escalations. Simple, fixed-rule tasks do not need this.

"Glue" departments

Teams like RevOps / DevOps / SecOps exist only because no single software owns a process that runs across departments. Automate that role and you capture a brand-new category of truth.
VIEW B · Mr. Girish Mathrubootham — Founder of Freshworks · early 2026 (a 3-part series)

"Build It Fresh for AI": redesign the software from the ground up

Main point: SaaS will not be killed by any single AI model. It will be disturbed by whoever rebuilds enterprise software from scratch for the AI era — where AI is the very foundation of the logic, not an add-on stuck on later.

Part 1 — Anthropic's agents will not finish off SaaS

Will "vibe coding" kill SaaS? No.

Yes, AI tools can now produce a working app in minutes. But that is only a weekend prototype — not a real system that can handle 10,000 users at once, pass SOC2 security audits, connect to 17 old systems, and survive a 3 a.m. emergency. And in any case, you would only be re-creating yesterday's form-filling software.

Agents versus the master system

If an agent sits on top of Salesforce or Freshworks to add intelligence, how does that kill the platform underneath? As he puts it: "The agent is the brain. The SaaS platform is the memory and the nervous system." Agents need both the structured data (from CRM/ERP) and the unstructured data (emails, Zoom, call recordings).
An important counter-point from him: Mr. Girish clearly rejects the idea that "you can throw away the master system and just let AI figure out everything from Zoom calls, emails and transcripts." He calls this "a very flawed assumption" and promises a separate write-up on why it will fail. (This is exactly the point where he and Foundation Capital pull in opposite directions — kindly see Section 4.)

So then, who will disturb SaaS? Not an AI release. Either (a) an existing big player brave enough to disrupt its own old design, or (b) an AI-first startup that builds AI as the core foundation — not as a wrapper.

Part 2 — Why companies cannot simply "Build" their own

Right now, every serious company is assembling its own AI system in-house, because there is no mature ready-made product to buy yet. Mr. Girish argues this is only temporary.

His analogy: "Every data team that set up its own Hadoop cluster in 2013 had moved to Snowflake or Databricks by 2019." The pattern always repeats — the basic open-source pieces become easily available → every company builds its own → then one proper product arrives that absorbs all the complexity → and the build-it-yourself crowd quietly shifts over to it.

Three reasons "Build it yourself" eventually loses

AI models change every quarter

Software written for last year's model looks nothing like what you will need in 2027.

The surrounding pieces change even faster

The agent frameworks, the memory layers, the testing tools — all are changing faster than the models themselves.

The unglamorous 20% takes years

Scale, security, compliance, monitoring, fine permission controls, serving many clients safely on one system (multi-tenancy) — this is what separates a demo from a real production product.

What will end the "build it yourself" phase is a new generation of AI-first enterprise products that absorb all this complexity on the customer's behalf.

Part 3 — Six features of the next-generation enterprise software

His blueprint (explained using CRM as the example, but he says it applies to every category):

His closing line: "Build it yourself" is winning today only because the mature ready-made option does not exist yet — and that window is closing faster than the big players realise. The winners "will not write AI features into a 2015 design — they will write the 2015 design out of existence."

4 · Putting both views together

Mr. Girish describes the visible product; Foundation Capital describes the hidden advantage underneath it.

  • Mr. Girish (the surface): what the AI-first winner looks and feels like — semantic master system, conversation interface, digital teammates, instant answers.
  • Foundation Capital (the moat): why it becomes impossible to dislodge — the private decision-history data that keeps growing with every run.

The strongest takeaway for a founder is to combine the two: build Mr. Girish's reimagined product, but make sure its lasting advantage is Foundation Capital's context graph. A good-looking AI-first CRM with no private decision data is still just a nicer wrapper. The real defensibility comes from the decision traces you collect by sitting at the moment of action — and you can start with a human-in-the-loop model (the agent proposes, collects context, routes approvals and records the trace) and automate more as the past-case library grows.

A combined test for any "AI agent for operations" idea

Ask yourself…From
Is this task full of exceptions and judgement, with real past-cases — where "it depends" is the honest answer? (Not a simple fixed-rule task.)A

5 · What a new player can take from this — the actionable bits

Stripping away the theory, here is what these two views actually tell someone building today:

Do thisWhy it works
Sit on top of the master system — don't replace itPlug into the existing CRM/ERP and write the final result back to it. Fastest path to value and the smallest, safest "yes" for a customer.
Pick an exception-heavy "glue" workflowDeal desks, underwriting, escalations, compliance, reconciliations — where "it depends" is the honest answer and many people work by hand. Avoid simple fixed-rule tasks; there is no lasting advantage there.
Capture the decision trace from day oneEven with a human approving each step, save every input, the policy applied, the exception, the approver and the reason. This accumulating "why" record is the real moat — and it grows with every run.
Build AI-native, not a wrapperDesign it for AI to read and reason from the start. A weekend prototype is easy to copy; production-grade AI-native software is not.
Win on the unglamorous 20%Security, compliance, multi-tenancy, audit trails, fine permissions. This is exactly what makes "buy" beat "build" — and what clones and in-house teams keep underestimating.
Sell an outcome, not softwareOffer a "digital teammate" with a clear scope and targets, not another tool to operate. Easier to buy, higher value, stickier.
Move now — the window is openCompanies are building their own only because no mature product exists yet. The first credible "buy" option in a niche usually wins the category.
The one-line strategy: pick a single exception-heavy "glue" workflow, sit on top of the existing master system, do the work AI-natively — and quietly accumulate the decision-trace data that becomes your moat. The AI models keep changing every quarter; the decision history that only you hold does not.

6 · Whose interest is it? (Both are partly talking their own book)

Foundation Capital

This is a venture-capital firm signalling what it wants to invest in. The "trillion-dollar / big players simply cannot do it" framing is deliberately bold, and it conveniently names companies of the type it backs (Regie, Maximor, PlayerZero, Arize). In reality, Salesforce and others are not as helpless as suggested.

Mr. Girish / Freshworks

This is a SaaS company founder and investor, with a strong interest in arguing that "SaaS survives" and that nimble rebuilders (such as a Freshworks) win — rather than the big AI-model labs. His "build always loses to buy" line is also convenient for product vendors.
Net-net: trust the overlapping mechanics (capturing data at the moment of action; rebuilding AI-first; software that does the work) more than either author's confident conclusion about who finally wins.

7 · Detailed word list

The three terms worth keeping in mind are at the very top of this page. Below are the few new concepts these essays introduce (not common jargon — these are the actual subject matter):

System of Record (SoR)
The single official master database the whole company trusts as final truth for a type of information — the CRM for customers, the HR system for employees. Owning it means owning the workflow and the customer lock-in.
Canonical data
The one official, agreed-upon version of a piece of information — no conflicting duplicate copies.
Moat
A durable advantage that stops competitors from copying you. The whole debate is about where the next one will sit.
Decision trace
A saved record of how one specific decision was taken: what inputs were gathered, which policy applied, which exception route was used, who approved, the reasoning, and the final result.
Context graph (View A's core idea)
The full connected web of decision traces, joined across the business over time — so past cases become searchable. This is the "why" layer.
Knowledge graph / map (View B's core idea)
A structured model of the business itself (entities, relationships, ideal customer, win/loss logic) that AI can reason over. This is the "what / who" layer.
Semantic system of record
A data store designed for AI to read and understand, instead of old databases designed for humans to fill in and read.
Execution (write) path vs. read path
Write path = where the work happens and the result is saved (agents sit here). Read path = where data is simply looked up later, after the event. Only the write path can see the "why" at the moment of decision.
