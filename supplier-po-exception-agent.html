The Supplier & Purchase-Order Exception Agent

An AI worker that watches every order a retailer places with its suppliers, catches the ones going wrong, chases the supplier, sorts out the paperwork, and only bothers a human when real judgement is needed. This is a full, plain-language explanation: how retail buying works, exactly where it breaks, what it costs, and what the agent does about it.

Every retailer buys stock from suppliers. Thousands of orders go out; most arrive fine. But a steady slice go wrong — late, short, mis-priced, or simply never confirmed. Today, a small team of people fixes these by hand, mostly through email and spreadsheets, and the misses quietly cost the business money: empty shelves, lost sales, overpaid invoices, and wasted hours. This document explains that problem in full, then explains the product that fixes it.

1 · The short version

Picture the most reliable person in a retailer's buying office — the one who knows which supplier always ships late, who chases the missing delivery before anyone notices, who spots that an invoice is £400 too high, and who never forgets to follow up. Now imagine that person as software that never sleeps, watches every single order at once, and does the chasing and checking automatically.

That is the product. It is not a dashboard that shows you problems and leaves you to fix them. It is a worker that does the work: it finds the order that is going wrong, gathers the facts, writes and sends the email to the supplier, reconciles the paperwork when they reply, and escalates to a human only when a real decision is needed — recording everything it did so the business gets smarter each time.

2 · How retail buying actually works (the "happy path")

To see where things break, you first need the normal flow. When a retailer restocks a product, six things happen in order. Each step usually produces a standard electronic document (the codes in brackets are the EDI numbers the industry uses — you don't need to memorise them):

1 · OrderRetailer sends a Purchase Order to the supplier EDI 850
→
2 · ConfirmSupplier acknowledges it — agrees the items, date, price EDI 855
→
3 · ShipSupplier sends an Advance Ship Notice EDI 856
→
4 · ReceiveGoods arrive; warehouse records what actually turned up Goods receipt
→
5 · BillSupplier sends an invoice EDI 810
→
6 · Match & payRetailer checks order vs delivery vs invoice, then pays

Step 6 is the important one. It is called the three-way match: the retailer checks that what was ordered = what arrived = what is being billed. If all three agree, the order is clean and the supplier gets paid. If any of them disagree — wrong quantity, wrong price, missing items — that order becomes an "exception."

The one idea to hold onto: the whole business of this product is exceptions — the orders where the happy path didn't happen. On a good day exceptions are a small percentage of orders. But a mid-market retailer places so many orders that even a small percentage is a daily flood of problems, each one needing a human to notice it, understand it, and fix it.

3 · Where it breaks — the exact pain points

Here is every common way an order goes wrong, what actually happens inside the retailer, and what it costs. These are the problems the agent exists to solve.

① The order is never confirmed

The retailer sends the PO… and hears nothing. The supplier hasn't acknowledged it. Did they get it? Can they fulfil it? Is the stock coming or not? Nobody knows. Often this is only discovered weeks later when the shelf is empty and someone asks "where's that order?"

Cost: a silent gap in the supply chain. The retailer is blind to a problem until it has already become a stockout.

② The supplier confirms something different from what you ordered

You ordered 500 units for the 1st; they confirm 300 units for the 15th, at a higher price. This is a "discrepancy on acknowledgement." Someone has to notice the change, decide whether it's acceptable, and either accept it, push back, or find the missing stock elsewhere.

Cost: if nobody catches it, you've planned your shelves and promotions around stock that was quietly cut in half.

③ The shipping notice (ASN) is late, missing, or wrong

The Advance Ship Notice is the single most error-prone document in the whole chain, and a late or wrong one is the number-one compliance problem across major retailers. When it's wrong, the warehouse doesn't know what's arriving, can't plan labour, and can't check the delivery properly when it lands.

Cost: chaos at the loading dock, delayed put-away, and — when the retailer is strict — automatic financial penalties (see ⑦).

④ The delivery doesn't match the paperwork (short, over, wrong, or damaged)

The boxes arrive but the contents don't match the ASN or the PO: 460 units instead of 500 (a "short ship"), the wrong variant, extra unordered stock, or damaged goods. Someone in receiving has to spot the gap, document it, and start a conversation with the supplier about credit or a re-ship.

Cost: you either pay for stock you didn't get, or you sell stock you can't replace, or you spend hours arguing the difference.

⑤ The invoice doesn't match (price or quantity)

The bill says £10.50 a unit; the PO said £9.80. Or it bills for 500 when only 460 arrived. The finance team can't pay it cleanly. They either hold it up (annoying the supplier) or — far too often, because checking is slow — they just pay it and quietly overpay.

Cost: direct money lost. Industry studies put the leakage from price/quantity mismatches and deductions at several percent of purchasing spend — pure margin, gone.

⑥ Late deliveries turn into empty shelves — the lost-sale problem

The most expensive failure of all. A late or missing order means the product isn't on the shelf (or the website) when a customer wants it. The customer doesn't wait — they buy from a competitor. This is worst at peak season, when both demand and supplier strain are highest, and when one empty shelf on the busiest week wipes out a disproportionate share of the year's profit.

Cost: lost revenue you never even see — there's no record of the sale that didn't happen. For seasonal retailers this is the single biggest hidden leak.

⑦ Compliance penalties left on the table (and avoidable ones paid)

Big retailers fine their suppliers automatically when the supplier ships late or sends a bad ASN. Mid-market retailers could do the same to recover the cost of supplier mistakes — but most don't have the systems to track who breached what, so they swallow the cost. (And when they supply other retailers, they get fined for the same mistakes and don't dispute the invalid ones.)

Cost: money you're entitled to recover from under-performing suppliers, never claimed — and invalid penalties you pay without challenge.

⑧ The knowledge lives in people's heads, not the system

"That supplier always ships two days late — order early." "For this brand, call Maria, don't email." None of this is written down. It lives in the memory of one or two experienced staff. When they're on holiday, or they leave, the knowledge leaves with them and the team re-learns the same lessons the hard way.

Cost: the business never compounds what it learns; every season feels like starting over; a key person leaving is a real risk.

⑨ At peak, the whole thing drowns

All of the above is manageable at normal volume. But order volume can triple in the run-up to a peak season, while the team stays the same size. Exceptions pile up faster than humans can work them, so they get triaged by panic — the loudest supplier gets chased, the quiet stockout gets missed — exactly when mistakes are most expensive.

Cost: the failures cluster on the highest-stakes weeks of the year, when there's no slack to absorb them.
The pattern across all nine: the retailer's main system (its ERP) can usually show you that something is off if you go and run a report — but it does nothing about it. It won't chase the supplier, won't read the reply, won't reconcile the mismatch, won't remember the lesson. As one industry guide puts it bluntly: "ERP-native reporting is passive — it shows what the data says now, but doesn't chase down missing information or act when an exception is detected." The doing is left entirely to people.

4 · Who feels this pain (and a day in their life)

The buyer of this product is whoever owns the flow of stock from suppliers into the business. At a mid-market retailer that's usually one clear person — a Head of Supply Chain, Merchandising / Buying Operations Manager, or Inventory Control Lead. They have the authority to say yes, and they feel the pain directly in their numbers (availability, margin, team workload).

Under them sits a small team of buying / supply-chain administrators — the people who actually do the chasing. This is their day:

8:30am — Open the ERP, run the "open purchase orders" report, export to a spreadsheet. Two hundred lines. Which ones are at risk? No way to tell at a glance, so start scanning.

9:00am — Begin emailing suppliers one by one. "Hi, chasing PO 44821, can you confirm ship date?" Copy, paste, change the number, send. Forty times.

11:00am — Receiving flags a short delivery. Dig out the PO, dig out the ASN, count the difference, email the supplier, start a credit claim. Update the spreadsheet.

2:00pm — Finance forwards three invoices that "don't match." Go line by line against the orders and the receipts to find the discrepancy. One is a genuine overcharge; two are fine but took 20 minutes each to prove.

4:30pm — A supplier finally replies to this morning's chase: shipment delayed a week. Now decide — wait, expedite, or source elsewhere? Ask the manager. Update the spreadsheet again.

5:30pm — Half the morning's chases still have no reply. They roll to tomorrow's list. The spreadsheet is the only record of any of it.

It's robotic, repetitive, reactive work — and the business depends on it being done well, every day, without anything slipping.

6 · What the agent actually does

The agent sits quietly on top of the systems the retailer already has — it reads their orders, the supplier documents, and the supplier email inbox — and it works the exception queue the way a great ops person would, but continuously and across every order at once. Six jobs:

#What it doesIn plain words
1Watches every orderIt tracks the full life of every PO — ordered, confirmed, shipped, received, invoiced — and knows at all times which stage each one is at.
2Catches the exception earlyThe moment an order drifts off the happy path — no confirmation by day 2, no shipping notice, a mismatch, a short delivery — it flags it instantly, instead of weeks later.
3Gathers the full contextIt automatically pulls together everything about that order: the PO, the shipping notice, the receipt, past emails, and the supplier's track record — so the situation is understood, not just detected.
4Takes the actionIt writes and sends the right message to the supplier ("PO 44821 isn't confirmed — please confirm ship date for 500 units"), asks the right question, and proposes a resolution. When the supplier replies, it reads the reply and continues the conversation.
5Reconciles the paperworkIt does the three-way match automatically — order vs delivery vs invoice — and surfaces only the genuine discrepancies, with the numbers already worked out, so nothing gets overpaid by default.
6Escalates & recordsWhen a real judgement call is needed (accept a delay? approve a price change?), it hands a human a clear recommendation, not a blank problem. Then it records what was decided and why — building the memory the business never had.
The key difference from everything that exists today: it doesn't just tell a person there's a problem and wait. It does the work — the watching, the chasing, the reading, the matching, the remembering — and brings a human in only for the small share of decisions that genuinely need one.

Human-in-the-loop, by design

It doesn't go rogue. Early on, it can be set to draft every supplier email for a quick human "send" — building trust. As it proves itself on the routine cases, more of them become fully automatic ("touchless"), while the judgement calls always route to a person. The retailer chooses how much rope to give it, and widens that as confidence grows.

7 · A worked example

A mid-sized toy retailer, six weeks before Christmas. A best-selling item is due in from a supplier on a PO placed three weeks ago.

Day 0 — The agent notices the PO has not been acknowledged. Old way: nobody would spot this until the stock failed to arrive. New way: the agent emails the supplier the same day asking for confirmation.

Day 1 — Supplier replies: they can only ship 60% of the order, and a week late. The agent reads this, recognises it as a shortfall on a high-demand seasonal line, and — because it knows this item is selling fast — escalates to the buying manager with a clear summary and a recommendation: "60% confirmed, one week late. At current sell-through this risks a stockout on the peak weekend. Options: (a) accept and re-order the gap from the alternate supplier, (b) push this supplier for full quantity."

Day 1, 2 minutes later — The manager picks option (a) with one click. The agent drafts the top-up order to the second supplier and the reply to the first.

Day 9 — Goods arrive. The agent matches the delivery to the shipping notice: 12 units short. It opens a credit claim with the supplier automatically and notes it.

Day 14 — The invoice comes in billing for the full original quantity. The agent catches the overcharge against the actual receipt, holds the payment, and sends the supplier the corrected figure with the evidence attached.

The result — The shelf stays full through peak. The retailer didn't overpay. And the agent has recorded the whole episode: this supplier under-ships seasonal lines, so next year it can flag that risk before the order even goes out.

The old way, the same story ends with an empty shelf on the busiest weekend, an overpaid invoice, and no record of why — and the lesson forgotten by next Christmas.

9 · What it deliberately does NOT do

A big reason this is buildable by a small team — and easy for a retailer to say yes to — is that it stays in one lane. It is not a giant platform. To be clear about the edges:

  • It does not decide what or how much to buy. That's demand forecasting and replenishment — already owned by big suites and a different, deeper problem. The agent takes the orders that have already been placed and makes sure they actually happen correctly.
  • It does not replace the ERP. It sits on top of the existing system and writes the clean result back to it. Low risk, fast to adopt, nothing ripped out.
  • It is not a new EDI network. It uses the documents flowing through whatever the retailer already has.
  • It is not a dashboard. Reports are a by-product; the point is the work it does, not another screen to watch.
One problem, solved to the core: "make sure every order a retailer places actually arrives correctly, on time, and is billed correctly — with as little human effort as possible." Narrow on purpose. That narrowness is what makes it easy to buy and hard to do badly.

10 · How you'd prove it works — a two-week pilot

The product earns trust by being measurable from day one. A simple, low-risk first test:

  • Scope: pick one slice — say the retailer's top 50 suppliers, or one product category — so the blast radius is tiny.
  • Access: read-only to start — the open orders, the shipping notices, the goods receipts, and the supplier emails for that slice. Nothing it can break.
  • What it does in the test: detect the exceptions, draft the supplier chases, do the three-way matches, and surface each issue with a recommended action.
  • The number that decides it: the "touchless resolution rate" — what share of exceptions it can handle end-to-end with no human keystrokes — plus the time saved and the £ of stockouts and overpayments it would have prevented, measured against two weeks of doing it the old way.
  • Green light: if it cleanly handles ~40–50%+ of that slice's exceptions on its own, the case to roll it wider makes itself.

11 · Why it gets stronger over time

A simple AI tool that drafts emails is easy to copy. What's hard to copy is the thing this agent quietly builds: a private record of how this retailer's supplier problems actually get solved. Every chased order, every accepted delay, every approved price change, every "this supplier always does X" — all of it is captured.

That record does two things. It makes the agent better — next season it can predict which suppliers will slip and act before the order is even placed. And it makes the agent sticky — the longer a retailer uses it, the more of their hard-won operational memory lives inside it, and the less they'd ever want to go back to spreadsheets and the knowledge walking out the door with a departing employee.

The compounding loop: more orders worked → more decisions recorded → smarter predictions and a deeper memory → more trust → more of the work handled automatically → and a moat that grows with every order, not something a competitor can clone in a weekend.

Glossary — the few terms used

Purchase Order (PO)
The retailer's official order to a supplier: which items, how many, by when, at what price.
Advance Ship Notice (ASN)
The supplier's heads-up of what's being shipped, in which boxes, arriving when. The most error-prone document, and the most common cause of penalties when it's wrong.
Goods receipt
The record the warehouse makes of what actually turned up when a delivery arrives.
Three-way match
Checking the order, the delivery, and the invoice against each other before paying. If they don't all agree, it's an exception.
Exception
Any order that didn't go smoothly — late, short, mis-priced, unconfirmed. Exceptions are the entire focus of this product.
Chargeback / deduction
A financial penalty for a supply-chain mistake (e.g. a late or wrong shipment), or a deduction taken off an invoice. Retailers can levy these on suppliers; mid-market retailers often fail to track and recover them.
EDI (Electronic Data Interchange)
The long-standing standard that lets retailer and supplier computers exchange order paperwork automatically. The numbers (850, 855, 856, 810) are just the codes for each document type.
ERP
The retailer's central system for stock, orders and finance. Good at storing data; does nothing on its own about exceptions.
Touchless resolution
An exception fully handled by the agent with no human action needed. The headline measure of how much work it's actually taking off people's plates.
